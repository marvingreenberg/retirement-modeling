## Outcome Distribution

The outcome distribution (fan chart) visualizes the range of possible portfolio outcomes from a Monte Carlo simulation. Instead of a single projected balance line, it shows bands representing different percentiles across all iterations.

### Percentile Bands

The chart displays five percentile lines, creating shaded bands between them:

- **95th percentile** (top edge) — Only 5% of simulations did better. Represents unusually favorable market sequences.
- **75th percentile** — A good outcome. Markets performed above average.
- **50th percentile (median)** — The middle outcome. Half of simulations ended higher, half lower. This is the most representative "typical" scenario.
- **25th percentile** — A below-average outcome. Markets underperformed.
- **5th percentile** (bottom edge) — Only 5% of simulations did worse. Represents poor market sequences, including major sustained downturns.

### How to Read It

**Width of the fan** indicates uncertainty. A narrow fan means outcomes are similar regardless of market conditions — the plan is robust. A wide fan means the plan is sensitive to market performance, and actual results could vary significantly.

**Median line** is the most useful reference point. If the median stays above zero through the full timeline, the plan succeeds in a "typical" market. If the median hits zero before the end, the plan fails more often than it succeeds.

**5th percentile** is the stress test. If this line stays above zero, the plan survives even in historically bad market sequences. If it hits zero early, there is meaningful risk of portfolio depletion — consider reducing spending, delaying retirement, or increasing savings.

**95th percentile** represents the upside. If this is substantially above the median, there is potential for significant surplus — which might mean the plan is more conservative than necessary.

### Practical Guidance

A plan where the 5th percentile depletes early but the median is healthy suggests a plan that works most of the time but is vulnerable to poor sequence-of-returns risk. The appropriate response depends on personal risk tolerance — some planners target 90% success rate (10th percentile stays positive), others target 95% or higher.
