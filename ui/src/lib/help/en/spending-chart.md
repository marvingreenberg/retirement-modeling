## Spending Chart

The spending chart shows the annual cash flow breakdown as a stacked bar chart. Each component of annual outflows is color-coded and stacked to show total cash needs per year.

### Components

<!-- if:has_base_spending -->
**Base Spending (blue)** — The core retirement spending amount, determined by the selected spending strategy. For fixed dollar, this is the inflation-adjusted target. For percent of portfolio, it varies with portfolio value.
<!-- endif -->
<!-- if:has_planned_expenses -->
**Planned Expenses (teal)** — One-time or recurring additional expenses beyond base spending (e.g., home repairs, travel, healthcare events). These appear only in the years they are scheduled.
<!-- endif -->
<!-- if:has_income_tax -->
**Income Tax (red)** — Federal and state income tax on withdrawals from pre-tax accounts, taxable income streams, and the taxable portion of Social Security benefits.
<!-- endif -->
<!-- if:has_conversion_tax -->
**Conversion Tax (orange)** — Tax incurred from Roth conversions. This is the income tax on the converted amount, shown separately from regular income tax to make the conversion cost visible.
<!-- endif -->
<!-- if:has_irmaa -->
**IRMAA (pink)** — Medicare premium surcharges triggered when income exceeds IRMAA thresholds. Shown as a distinct cost component because it represents a cliff-based penalty that can be avoided through income management.
<!-- endif -->
<!-- if:has_surplus -->
**Surplus (green)** — Income that exceeds spending needs in a given year. This is routed to an investment account per the excess income routing setting. Common in pre-retirement years when employment income exceeds spending.
<!-- endif -->

### Desired Spending Line

When using the fixed dollar strategy, a **dashed line** shows the inflation-adjusted desired spending amount. The actual base spending should track this line. If actual spending falls below the dashed line, it indicates the portfolio could not fully fund the target (partial spending shortfall).

### Reading the Chart

Tall bars in early retirement often reflect high conversion tax (aggressive Roth conversions) or surplus income (still working). The transition to steady-state spending typically occurs once income streams end and conversion activity stabilizes. A growing income tax component in later years may indicate rising RMD obligations.
